# Dungeon Steward Naming Lexicon

## Document Status
- Status: Working Draft
- Last Updated: 2026-04-06
- Scope: naming rules for cards, relics, enemies, bosses, places, factions, and institutions
- Depends On: `design/world/canon-foundation.md`, `design/world/factions-and-cultures.md`, `design/world/conflict-seeds.md`, `design/art-bible.md`

## Naming Goals
1. Preserve readability first.
2. Make the world feel administratively courtly, materially grounded, and frontier-specific.
3. Let names carry social role, material texture, and faction identity.
4. Avoid collapsing back into generic fantasy sludge or overlong purple prose.

## Core Naming Policy
- Common combat cards may stay short and plain when clarity wins. `Strike`, `Defend`, and `Scheme` are acceptable readability anchors.
- World flavor should increase as content becomes more specific, higher rarity, event-facing, relic-facing, boss-facing, or socially situated.
- A flavorful name should still be concrete. Prefer an object, action, office, tool, or relationship over abstract mystical fog.

## Card Naming Policy

### Baseline readability lane
- 1-2 words preferred
- direct action beats deep lore
- ideal for starter cards, common defense, common attacks, and tutorial-facing content

Examples:
- Strike
- Defend
- Scheme
- Brace
- Salvage Pull
- Gate Hold

### Flavor-forward lane
- 2-3 words preferred
- one vivid noun plus one practical verb or role word
- ideal for advanced cards, reward variants, hybrid cards, and event-linked cards

Examples:
- Warrant Anchor
- Velvet Riposte
- Sluice Patch
- Rosette Verdict
- Gate Sanction
- Seep Splice

### Rule for flavorful card names
If a name cannot be imagined as a thing someone in the world could plausibly say, carry, sign, perform, or survive, it is probably too vague.

## Relic Naming Policy
Relics may carry more flavor than cards, but should still feel materially real.

Preferred relic nouns:
- medallion
- bell
- clasp
- seal
- key
- token
- warrant
- clasp-knife
- ledger seal
- gate pin

## Boss and Enemy Naming Policy
- enemies should name either role, function, or failure mode
- bosses should sound like concentrated institutions, ideologies, or threshold forces
- avoid random-syllable fantasy names unless tied to a strong faction lexicon later

Enemy examples:
- Tunnel Inspector
- Spill Siphoner
- Rival Bailiff
- Gate Deserter
- Sluice Raider

Boss examples:
- The Charter Husk
- Moonlit Adjudicator
- Warden of the Breach Gate
- Sovereign Under the Seal

## Place and Institution Patterns
Preferred place structures:
- [function] + [place] -> Watch Gate, Sluice Court, Threshold Hall
- [material/social noun] + [civic noun] -> Seal House, Charter Hall, Ribbon Court
- [danger noun] + [frontier noun] -> Breach Post, Hollow Gate, Undergate Works

## Current Approved Exemplars
Existing card names that already sit in a usable lane:
- Ember Jab
- Ward Polish
- Split Cut
- Shell Brace
- Vault Focus
- Seam Pull
- Contract Anchor

These still lean mechanical, but they point toward a good direction: short, concrete, and role-aware.

## Forbidden Lanes
- vague high-fantasy noun sludge (`Arcane Destiny of the Forgotten Depths`-type naming)
- meme/comedy wording
- modern snark
- overlong boss titles that lose immediate readability
- romance-coded or coercion-coded charm vocabulary

## Quick QA Check Before Approving a Name
1. Can a player read it quickly in combat?
2. Does it imply an action, object, office, or danger?
3. Does it sound like Dungeon Steward rather than generic fantasy?
4. Does it belong to a recognizable lane?
5. Is the flavor adding role clarity, not just decoration?
