# Dungeon Steward World Canon Foundation

## Document Status
- Status: Working Draft
- Last Updated: 2026-04-06
- Scope: smallest viable world foundation for gameplay-facing flavor and future world expansion
- Primary Inputs: `design/gdd/game-concept.md`, `design/art-bible.md`, `tools/imagegen/prompts/dungeon_steward_character_faction_prompt_pack.md`

## Purpose
This document defines the north star of Dungeon Steward's setting without pretending the world is more fully solved than it is. It exists to answer:
- what is actually true in the setting right now
- what people in the setting only believe or say
- what tone and material language the world must preserve
- what open questions should remain unresolved rather than quietly hardening into canon

## Core Setting Premise
Dungeon Steward takes place at a chartered frontier outpost where ceremony, paperwork, favors, and practical survival are all tangled up with a nearby dungeon. The protagonist is not a generic wanderer but a steward: part official, part duelist, part social broker, and part field problem-solver. The world should feel like a civic machine trying to remain elegant while sitting next to something dangerous.

## Truth vs Belief Split

### Truth: current objective setting anchors
1. The frontier is governed through charters, inspections, warrants, recognized offices, and semi-formal lines of authority.
2. The dungeon is materially real, dangerous, and economically consequential. It is not just a mythic backdrop.
3. Multi-species society is normal to the setting, not a novelty event. Cats, badgers, goblins, pard elves, and others occupy intelligible social roles.
4. Social bonds, favors, office, and reputation have practical force. They are not merely decorative flavor.
5. Charm in Dungeon Steward is social-magical affiliation, favor, attraction, duty, or willing entanglement. It is not coercive enthrallment or loss-of-agency mind control.
6. Civic order in the frontier is always under strain from the dungeon, scarcity, politics, labor realities, and unequal status between groups that still need each other.
7. Cards are framed as favors, warrants, procedures, prepared techniques, and named allies as much as raw attacks or spell effects.

### Belief: common in-world stories that may be partial, self-serving, or false
- The charter-house makes the frontier civilized and keeps the dungeon governable.
- Badger wardens are the hard line between order and total collapse.
- Seep-goblin improvisation is indispensable, but never fully respectable.
- Pard-elf grace and severity signal superior judgment, lineage, and fitness to rule.
- A talented steward can turn dangerous social entanglements into useful public order.
- Procedure can eventually tame any frontier disorder if the right people hold the seals, keys, and warrants.

These beliefs are useful world pressure. They should not automatically be treated as truth.

## Canon Tiers
- Core Canon: stable enough to build systems, naming, enemies, and hub flavor on top of.
- Working Canon: currently approved framing that may be refined as more content is authored.
- Flavor: motifs and examples that support the tone but do not define world law.
- Open Question: material that must remain explicitly unresolved.

## Tone Pillars
1. Elegant absurdity
   The world is strange, but it treats itself seriously. Humor comes from premise and sincerity, not parody.

2. Ceremony beside danger
   The world uses seals, ribbons, warrants, velvet, bells, medallions, gates, ledgers, and inspections to hold itself together next to a live dungeon.

3. Readable civic fantasy
   The setting should feel distinctive without becoming mushy, over-ornamented, or too abstract to support gameplay clarity.

4. Persons, not mascots
   Every species or faction must read as a people with motivations, blind spots, and social logic rather than a one-note gag.

5. Combat-supportive world flavor
   The world should feed combat, rewards, enemies, bosses, relics, and hub choices rather than demanding a separate novel's worth of exposition.

## Anti-Tone Guardrails
- No generic mud-fantasy sludge.
- No meme collage, broad parody, or clownish species handling.
- No coercive charm framing.
- No modern bureaucratic literalism that erases the fantasy register.
- No ornate world detail that weakens combat readability or card identity.

## Current World Rules
1. Authority is layered, not absolute.
   Formal charters and warrants matter, but frontier survival still depends on informal favors, negotiated trust, and whoever can actually keep gates, tunnels, and works functioning.

2. Materials carry social meaning.
   Brass, iron, parchment, wax, lacquer, velvet, satin, rope, roughhide, scarred wood, bottle-glass, and controlled rune-teal magic all signal who a person is and what part of the frontier machine they belong to.

3. The dungeon leaks pressure into civic life.
   The frontier does not merely sit near danger. It organizes itself around danger: inspections, maintenance, vigilance, sealed authorities, and tactical preparedness all exist because adjacency to the dungeon is normal and costly.

4. Every faction must justify itself in public language.
   The world should produce self-explanations like duty, stewardship, service, elegance, necessity, or sacrifice. No major group should feel like it exists only because the author wanted a cool costume.

5. World flavor should map onto gameplay roles.
   Stewards broker, certify, and redirect. Wardens brace, inspect, and enforce. Goblin crews patch, salvage, and improvise. Pard-elf figures signal prestige, judgment, and severe refinement. Dungeon pressures produce breaches, sovereign threats, spillovers, and threshold crises.

6. Cards must feel like instruments of office, favor, craft, or technique.
   Even when a card is mechanically simple, the world framing should support the fantasy that the player is using prepared means within a real social-material order.

## Gameplay-Facing Implications
- Enemy families should emerge from civic and dungeon pressures, not only from monster taxonomy.
- Bosses should feel like institutions, ideologies, or threshold forces pushed into embodied conflict.
- Relics should read as court-frontier objects: seals, warrants, medallions, bells, keys, pins, ledgers, charter tools, and sanctioned oddities.
- Hub investments should feel like administrative, infrastructural, or social changes rather than abstract menu upgrades.
- Events can draw from inspections, petitions, repairs, rival claims, courtly debts, tunnel incidents, and controlled breaches.

## Current Core Canon Statements
- Dungeon Steward is a frontier stewardship fantasy, not a pure murderhobo dungeon crawl.
- The hub is best understood as a charter-house or related civic outpost rather than a generic camp.
- The deck can plausibly contain both procedural and personal instruments: warrants, techniques, favors, named helpers, and prepared responses.
- Courtly style and frontier grime must coexist in the same world without canceling each other out.
- The setting's species/faction mix should create social texture, not a random anthology of cool creatures.

## Open Questions Promoted from Working Canon
1. What exact legal or political authority does a steward hold over the frontier and the dungeon approach?
2. What does the dungeon produce, threaten, distort, or attract besides obvious combat danger?
3. Is the charter-house part of a larger state, a licensed private order, or a hybrid civic-commercial institution?
4. How formal are charm markers and favors: court custom, legal token, ritual signal, or overlapping mix?
5. Are pard elves local power-holders, external elite houses, or prestige specialists embedded into the frontier?
6. What makes a boss a boss in-world: office, corruption, scale, sacred threshold status, or political centrality?
7. Which frontier groups exist outside the charter-house order and contest its legitimacy?
