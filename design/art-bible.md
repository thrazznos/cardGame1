# Art Bible: Dungeon Steward

## Document Status
- **Version**: 0.1
- **Last Updated**: 2026-04-05
- **Owned By**: art-director
- **Status**: Draft
- **Scope Note**: This first pass is character/faction-first and establishes the visual north star for portraits, cards, and UI skinning before full environment production.

## Visual Identity Summary
Dungeon Steward presents a browser-first fantasy strategy game through the lens of deadpan fantasy absurdism: a serious, tactically readable world whose absurd species and social arrangements are treated as entirely normal. The player is a courtly feline steward-charmer and duelist, and the deck is framed as a portable case of favors, introductions, warrants, and named allies drawn from a bizarre but coherent frontier society.

The visual goal is not parody, meme-collage, or thirstbait spectacle. The target is elegant absurdity: courtly glamour, restrained ornament, strong silhouettes, clear material language, and character portraits that remain legible at card scale while preserving a distinctive cast identity.

## Art Style

### Rendering Style
- **Primary style**: stylized 2D fantasy with pixel-adjacent discipline rather than painterly sprawl
- **Portrait priority**: card art should read as illustrated portrait plates, not full action scenes
- **Humor model**: straight-faced absurdism; absurd subjects rendered with formal seriousness
- **Visual density**: moderate; details must support the first read rather than replace it

### Proportions
- Characters are stylized but not chibi.
- Heads, shoulders, hands, and one key prop must remain readable at card scale.
- Portrait composition should favor bust-to-waist or bust-to-knee framing over full-body action scenes.
- Top-third readability is critical: species, face, ears/hair/horns, shoulder silhouette, and primary prop should identify the card immediately.
- UI scale relationships must prioritize numbers, state chips, and rules text over decorative art.

### Level of Detail
- Common cards: one clear prop, one strong faction cue, one readable costume hook.
- Uncommon cards: slightly richer costume layering and stronger environmental framing.
- Rare / boss cards: more dramatic composition, higher contrast, and richer ornament, but still only one primary read.
- Backgrounds in card art must remain simplified and low-competition.
- Patterns and translucency should be handled as bold graphic reads, not fine texture noise.

### Visual Hierarchy
1. Gameplay truth first: cost, legality, states, and effects must always outrank ornament.
2. Face and silhouette second: players should recognize card identity quickly.
3. Faction / charm markers third: these provide theme and recall.
4. Ornament last: decoration must frame information, never bury it.

Visual hierarchy rule:
- If a decorative element competes with text, numbers, state chips, or silhouette recognition, the decorative element loses.

## Character Art Standards
Character art exists to support three simultaneous reads:
1. mechanical role,
2. faction/species identity,
3. social relationship to the protagonist.

### Global Character Rules
- Every character must read as a person first, a joke second.
- Humor arises from premise, sincerity, and social framing — never from broken anatomy or clownish exaggeration.
- Each portrait should answer four questions quickly:
  - What is this person's role?
  - What species/faction are they?
  - How dangerous or useful are they?
  - How are they connected to the cat steward-charmer?
- Shared charm marker should unify the cast without erasing faction identity.

### Shared Charm Marker
Recommended standard:
- **Primary marker**: oxblood ribbon, knot, or favor
- **Secondary marker**: tarnished-gold clasp, brooch, seal, or pin
- **Optional rare accent**: restrained rune-teal detail for higher rarity or stronger magical alignment

Allowed placements:
- lapel favor
- scabbard tie
- hair braid token
- charm clasp
- harness fastening
- pinned flower or seal
- floating anchor charm for non-solid species

Charm rule:
- The marker implies allegiance, fascination, office, favor, or willing entanglement.
- It must **not** imply hypnosis, loss of agency, or coercive possession.

### Protagonist: Courtly Cat Steward-Charmer / Duelist
Core read:
- feline-first, poised, dangerous, socially magnetic
- a licensed frontier official whose charm is procedural as well as personal

Silhouette:
- upright feline body language
- narrow duelist frame
- distinct ears, tail, whiskers, and rapier-line posture
- strong verticality with subtle asymmetry from cloak, tail, or layered accessories

Costume language:
- velvet
- satin lining
- fitted duelist coat
- gloves
- signet details
- polished light leather
- precise jewelry, not cluttered jewelry

Expression:
- composed confidence
- precise amusement
- quiet social predation
- never “internet cat chaos”

Hard guardrails:
- do not reduce the protagonist to generic anime cat-person styling
- do not overburden with pirate clutter or fussy baroque noise
- attractiveness should come from poise, intelligence, and control

### Elves: Purple Leopard-Print Variety
Recommended in-world tone:
- aristocratic, predatory, moonlit, self-possessed
- leopard patterning is biological and prestigious, not novelty fashion

Silhouette:
- tall, long-limbed, exacting posture
- blade-like ears and long necks
- elegant robes, capes, sabers, spears, or bows
- clothing should frame the body's natural patterning rather than bury it

Palette:
- lavender to deep plum
- orchid-black
- wine-purple
- moon-silver
- blackberry near-black for pattern contrast

Pattern rules:
- rosettes must be bold, clean, and selectively placed
- visible anchor zones: face edge, shoulder, upper arm, collarbone, thigh, or cloak lining
- do not layer competing spotted textures across skin, fabric, and background simultaneously

Guardrails:
- no tacky all-over leopard chaos
- no “purple cat elf” confusion with the protagonist
- they must read as elves with predatory grace and court lineage

### Goblins: Slime Girls / Slime-Bodied Goblins
Core read:
- goblins first: cunning, improvised, useful, opportunistic
- slime-bodied second: fluid, glossy, unstable, adaptive
- feminine-coded presentation is valid, but must remain subordinate to species identity and readability

Silhouette:
- compact to medium scale
- clear head/core plus elastic edges
- readable face plane and one solid accessory block are mandatory
- asymmetry encouraged for scavenger-engineer energy

Palette:
- slime mint
- acid green
- seafoam
- cyan accents
- petroleum teal
- rusted metal / bottle-glass / dirty silver hardware

Material language:
- translucent gel
- internal suspended inclusions
- corked vials
- mesh and utility straps
- aprons, cropped coats, salvage rigs
- waterproof satchels and cheap chrome

Guardrails:
- avoid pin-up framing
- avoid “they are basically unclothed because slime” logic
- preserve goblin cleverness, municipal practicality, and tactical role clarity
- body translucency must never erase silhouette readability

### Sentient Honeybadgers / Settfolk / Badger Wardens
Core read:
- compact menace, practical gear, total refusal to be impressed
- the joke is that they are exactly as formidable and stubborn as expected, and the world treats this as an administrative fact

Silhouette:
- squat or low-center-of-gravity mass
- wedge head
- thick forearms
- strong dorsal stripe or back value split
- blocky utility profile even when upright

Palette:
- dirty cream
- soot black
- mud brown
- iron red
- wax amber

Material language:
- roughhide
- rope
- iron plates
- dented mail
- scarred wood
- utility harnesses
- practical scarves, gloves, belts, and vests

Guardrails:
- do not make them mascot-cute
- do not lean on meme expressions
- they are funny because they are dead serious, lawful, and very difficult to stop

### Card Portrait Composition Rules
- Prefer bust, waist-up, or bust-to-knee portraits.
- One focal face/figure plus one major prop or sigil.
- Backgrounds should be simple planes, symbolic backplates, or lightly framed contexts.
- Full battlefield action scenes should be reserved for rare/boss or event cards only.
- Every card portrait must survive a thumbnail test at actual hand-card size.

## Environment Art Standards
This first pass is character/faction-first, but the environment language must support the cast.

### World Read
- The frontier is not generic mud-fantasy; it is a chartered outpost of ceremony, paperwork, and survival built on top of something dangerous.
- The world should feel like a civic machine trying to remain elegant while sitting next to a dungeon.

### Environment Priorities
- warm lamplight vs cool stone
- candle amber interiors vs steel-blue dungeon pressure
- parchment, seals, bells, ledgers, ribbons, lacquer, and brass meeting rope, salvage, iron, and carved stone
- environments should support court-retinue logic without becoming palace excess

### Key Spaces
- charter-house halls
- parlor / salon-like decision spaces
- frontier offices, storage, and watch posts
- sluices, workshops, and goblin-maintained utility spaces
- badger-enforced tunnels, inspections, and gates
- moonlit or dusk-biased elf-influenced thresholds

### Environment Guardrails
- no generic tavern sludge if the scene is meant to convey courtly structure
- no ornate excess that obscures pathing or UI readability
- background textures must remain quieter than character silhouettes and UI text surfaces

## UI Art Standards
Dungeon Steward is still a browser-first strategy game. UI must remain sober, readable, and deterministic even when themed.

### General Principles
- Mechanics-first layout, theme-second ornament
- Decorative language should frame, not compete with, information
- Numbers, chips, states, and explanation surfaces remain plain and forceful
- Theme enters through trim, crests, panels, motifs, and portrait framing

### Card UI Standards
- Use one base layout for all cards.
- Text must live on controlled-contrast panels.
- `icon_key` should be reserved for reusable mechanical/category glyphs, not for full portrait art.
- `frame_style` should encode family/style lane, not runtime state.
- Rarity and runtime state should be orthogonal systems layered on top of the same readable structure.

Recommended card layout:
- portrait window at top
- large cost medallion
- rules text panel in the middle
- footer rail for tags/states
- small crest / rarity accent in a predictable corner

### HUD Standards
- Use restrained courtly chrome: ribbons, medallions, seals, enamel trims.
- Keep queue/order chips, legality feedback, sync banners, and combat explanations blunt and high-contrast.
- Resource HUD, enemy intent strip, and outcome badges must be mechanically obvious before they are themed.

### Small Symbol Rule
The following must be manually authored and pixel-tested:
- gameplay icons
- map node icons
- state badges
- queue chips
- tutorial prompt badges
- lock / pending / owned / unavailable symbols

AI may assist with motif ideation, but final small symbols must be hand-finished.

### Rarity / State Rules
- Common: modest metal/accent treatment
- Uncommon: stronger trim or enamel cue
- Rare: richer accent and cleaner glow/hierarchy
- Boss/special: stronger crest and composition, same core layout

Runtime states must never rely on color alone:
- playable
- selected
- unaffordable
- illegal target
- retained
- temporary / generated
- resolving / locked

### UI Guardrails
- no text over noisy art
- no over-ornamented frame that shrinks rules readability
- no tiny symbol system generated raw by AI
- no style choice that weakens colorblind-safe signaling

## VFX Standards
VFX should support sequencing clarity and social-magical identity.

### VFX Language
- charm-work should feel precise, ceremonial, and controllable
- use ribbons, seals, bell-rings, sigils, and restrained rune-teal highlights for charm/favor effects
- impact effects should remain short, readable, and shaped by role:
  - attack = sharp, diagonal, cutting
  - defense = compact, stable, layered
  - support/charm = circular, tethered, or seal-like
  - unstable goblin/slime utility = elastic, spill-like, but never visually ambiguous

### Motion Rules
- motion must reinforce order, cause, and effect
- long, foggy, or screen-filling effects are unacceptable if they hide combat state
- boss and rare cards may receive stronger spectacle, but the event order must remain readable

### VFX Guardrails
- no screen wash that hides queue/order information
- no faction effect that becomes unreadable in grayscale or reduced-motion mode
- no slime or rune effect that dissolves silhouettes during active decision windows

## Asset Production Standards

### Naming Convention
`[category]_[name]_[variant]_[size].[ext]`

Examples:
- `char_cat_steward_duelist_portrait_md.png`
- `char_elf_pard_duelist_portrait_md.png`
- `char_goblin_seep_alchemist_portrait_md.png`
- `char_honeybadger_warden_portrait_md.png`
- `ui_card_frame_court_base_md.png`
- `ui_icon_damage_sm.png`
- `env_charter_house_hall_bg_lg.png`

### Asset Family Prefixes
- `char_` — characters and portraits
- `ui_` — UI chrome, icons, badges, frames, panels
- `env_` — environments and backplates
- `vfx_` — particles and effects
- `obj_` — relics, props, symbolic object art

### Texture Standards
| Category | Max Resolution | Format | Color Space |
| -------- | -------------- | ------ | ----------- |
| Characters | 1536x1536 source, 512x512 runtime portrait target | PNG | sRGB |
| Environments | 2048x1152 source, export as needed for browser-safe backplates | PNG | sRGB |
| UI | author at 2x intended display size; export atlas-friendly assets | PNG | sRGB |
| VFX | 1024x1024 source ceiling per sheet/effect cluster | PNG | sRGB |

Notes:
- Source generation outputs may exceed runtime size during exploration, but curated runtime assets must be reduced to browser-appropriate dimensions.
- Final runtime portraits should be tested directly at actual card size before approval.

### Animation Standards
- Static portraits and panel systems first; animation is secondary.
- Use subtle motion only where it improves information hierarchy.
- UI animation should favor fade, slide, raise, pulse, and seal/crest emphasis over large sweeps.
- Reduced-motion compatibility is required from the outset.

### AI-Assisted Art Workflow Standard
AI generation is approved for:
- portrait ideation
- faction/concept sheets
- hub mood pieces
- relic/object ideation
- rare/boss splash bases

AI generation is **not** approved as the final source of truth for:
- icons
- badges/chips
- small state markers
- frame templates
- typography
- any asset whose primary job is to convey gameplay meaning at small size

Promotion rule:
- raw generations are exploratory
- only curated, cleaned, palette-normalized, and readability-tested assets may enter the approved asset library

## Accessibility
- Colorblind-safe UI elements required
- Minimum text size: **16px at 1080p** for core body/UI text; anything smaller requires explicit justification and testing
- High contrast mode specifications:
  - text panels use controlled flat-value backing
  - gameplay-critical states require icon + text/shape, not color alone
  - runtime overlays must remain readable in grayscale checks
- Icon + color (never color alone) for game state
- Portrait art must not be required to infer gameplay truth
- Reduced-motion mode must preserve all sequencing and legality information
- Thumbnail/card-size testing is required for every portrait family before approval

## Non-Negotiable Guardrails
- This project is a strategy deckbuilder first, not a romance game, dating sim, or meme collage.
- Charm must read as social-magical allegiance, not coercive enthrallment.
- Slime-goblin designs must preserve goblin utility and readability over pin-up framing.
- Honeybadgers must be credible people, not mascots.
- Purple leopard patterning must remain elegant and selective, never texture chaos.
- If a thematic decision harms browser readability, gameplay clarity wins.
